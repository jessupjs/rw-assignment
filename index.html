Covid-19 has impacted public spaces in different ways. We'll take a look at the toll on social confidence now and towards the November presidential election.

After months of wading the coronavirus info stream, many Americans have self-constructed their own safety measures. We surveyed individuals from different demographics, including age, gender, region and political lean, in order to better understand how people feel about being present in commonly peopled places. Though still months away from the election, we also peered into a possible scenario at polling stations.

Photo by Morning Brew on Unsplash

Going outside your home at all?

More than in any other activity we surveyed, results indicate that people feel relatively safe venturing outdoors. Can simply stepping outside be considered a social exercise? It may depend on whether you live on a 'block' or on a 'street' (the classic urban/rural debate).

Eating at a restaurant or drinking at a bar inside?

If a mask cover, open air, and a 6-foot people shield represent best practice, indoor dining might be demonstrate the worst (or 'riskiest' according to the CDC). Positions range by age and region, and individuals who political parties that champion person freedom (Libertarians, Republicans) feel safest.

Eating at a restaurant or drinking at a bar outside?

Unsurprisingly, confidence rises for outdoor dining, though this survey does not indicate whether the tables are in a sparse layout. It has been a scorching summer - as fall approaches this may be a category to watch as diners seek shelter from the cold.

Taking public transportation?

For people who still have to work onsite, public transportation may not be an optional risk, particularly in urban areas. There is a broad consensus that public transport is not safe, but interestingly in the population dense North East, higher numbers may indicate that some feel the worst is over.

Going shopping?

It can be hard to break the spirit of the world's #1 consumer economy, even if almost everything can be purchased online. Even so, many Americans (most notably suporters of President Trump), feel safe (second to just being outside anywhere) inside stores.

Going to the gym?

Exercise-induced huffing and puffing is a broad concern for most people, but the gym may lean more into a 'luxury' category for virus libertines that often yields slightly more approval from the right and from the young.

Isolation has been one of the hardest consequences of a restricted lifestyle, so it is perhaps understandable that friends still meet, hopefully with some candor about risk. Again we see the expected reservation from Democrats and those among the most vulnerable to the disease, the elderly.

Attending a sporting event?

This question could have been split in two - indoor and outdoor - but whether a packed stadium or arena, people will be shouting (or cheering - not always are they the same). While there are those who feel safe, this is probably one of the 'luxuries' that people don't mind watching from home.

Watching a movie at a movie theater?

Interestingly filling movie theater seats was already a concern even before the pandemic, and now some films are going straight to the competition: streaming services. People do not feel safe now - this may become an industry that does not survive the lockdown.

Taking a flight?

Airlines are another industry threatened by a severe ticket drop (although major airlines are receiving massive government support). Anecdotes are mixed: some fliers have praised effective distancing policies, while others felt frighteningly packed in - making it an experience that leave potential passengers feeling very uncertain.

Going to the hospital for something unrelated to coronavirus?

As a likely destination for those experiencing severe symptoms from the coronavirus, many hospitals have earned public trust by taking the threat seriously and implementing smart safety measure. Opinions are split in directions consistent with other public spaces, but the ratio safe to unsafe is closer to 50 / 50 than other categories.
